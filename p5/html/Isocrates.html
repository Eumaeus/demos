1. Isocrates

Born 436 BCE, died 338 BCE (source for date: OCD 3 ). Isocrates was the child of a wealthy flute-manufacturer who gave him an excellent education. He is said to have studied under Prodicus, Gorgias, Tisias, Theramenes, and Socrates. Isocrates spent the decade of the 390s writing speeches. However, much of the rest of Isocrates’ life was dedicated to teaching; he established a school at Athens sometime before 386 BCE (source for date: OCD 3 ). Among his students were the orators Hyperides and Isaeus, the historians Ephorus and Theopompus, and the atthidographer Androtion. Isocrates’ works are characterised by expressions of great love of Athens and by the promotion of ideas of Panhellenism, whereby, in his view, all of the Greek peoples could unite and declare war on the barbarous Persians. Of his works, twenty-one discourses on a wide range of topics and nine letters survive, most of which are considered to be genuine. (See also the genre of Oratory.)
