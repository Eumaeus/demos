1. Dem. 7

(Demosthenes, On Halonnesus, see also Oratory). This speech is somewhat controversial. The ancient commentator Dionysius of Halicarnassus calls it the Eighth Philippic (Dion. Hal. Ad Amm. 10.33; see also, Dem. 4, Dem. 6, Dem. 9, Dem. 10, the Philippics). But Libanius, another ancient commentator, says that this speech is not actually by Demosthenes, but by Hegesippus, based on its style and subject matter (Lib. Or. 7.6-8; unfortunately, no other speech by Hegesippus survive, so we cannot compare the styles for ourselves). The relevant texts from Dionysius and Libanius are given below. The background to the speech is this: Shortly after 346 BCE, Halonnesus, an island in the Aegean that belonged to Athens, was captured by a pirate named Sostratus. Philip of Macedon defeated this pirate and took over control of the island himself. In 343 BCE Athens sent an embassy to Philip to demand that he return Halonnesus to its rightful owner, Athens. One of the ambassadors from Athens was Hegesippus. Philip offered, not to return the island to Athens, but to give it as a gift. This speech, probably written by Demosthenes, but certainly not delivered by him — the speaker says that he was a member of the embassy to Philip, which Demosthenes was not (see Dem. 7.2) — was probably delivered before the Assembly in 342 BCE.

On the subject of Dem. 7, Dionysius of Halicarnassus says (Dion. Hal. Ad Amm. 10.33):

After Lykiskon was archon, Pythodotos was archon, in the year when the eighth of the speeches against Philip was composed, the beginning of which is ‘O men of Athens, there is no way that the charges…’

(compare Dem. 7.1) (
